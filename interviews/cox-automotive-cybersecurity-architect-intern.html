How I Got the Cox Automotive Cybersecurity Intern Offer — The Full Panel Q&A

A four-interviewer panel, nine questions, one hour. Here's every question they asked me, what I actually said, and the moments I think pushed the conversation in my favor.

Fall 2025 Offer received ~ 12 min read

Offer extended

Overview

This was my first true panel interview — not a one-on-one, not a screening call, but four senior security leaders on the same Zoom at the same time. If you've never done one, the jump from 1:1 to 4:1 is real. You're not just answering a question; you're answering it in a way that works for four different people with four different lenses.

I walked in thinking it would feel like an interrogation. It didn't. The Director of Architecture set the tone in the first minute — intros on their end, pass it to me, nine questions, time for my own questions at the end, and a promise it would be "relatively painless." It was. The format made room for real conversations, and that ended up being the difference.

Below is every question they asked me, the key insight I was trying to land, and — if you want the full version — the complete answer as I gave it. The four questions I asked them at the end are there too, and honestly, those might have been the most important moments of the whole hour.

Meet the Panel

Every question you read below came from one of these four people. Who asked matters — it tells you where the question is coming from and why it was worth asking.

Director of Integrated Cybersecurity Architecture
Led the session. His team partners with product and engineering groups that aren't security — acting as consultants on compliance, controls, and secure implementation.
Cybersecurity Architect
Reports to the Director. Day-to-day work is translating policies into concrete protective and detective controls with engineering teams.
Compliance Lead
Owns regulatory obligations — PCI, GLBA, internal/external audits. Makes sure the security bar is met and can be attested to.
Director of Continuous Controls Monitoring
Runs the engineering team that builds the "lens" into security compliance — visibility into how teams are doing against patching, EOL, secure coding, and configuration requirements.

The Conversation

Nine questions total. I've grouped them by theme rather than strict chronological order — technical foundations first, then motivation and self-awareness, then how I work, and finally one of the most revealing questions a panel can ask.

Technical foundations

Q1

"Tell me about your AppSec internship — especially a scenario where there was a vulnerability and you had to recognize the level of risk. How did you go about it?" Asked by: Director of Architecture

// key insight

A 9.5-severity RCE had been sitting in a team's codebase for five years because nobody had re-scanned legacy dependencies. The fix wasn't just closing the bug — it was setting up hourly scans with three-hour result pushes so the dev team could see blockers in near-real-time without me being a meeting in the middle. The real win was making security visible instead of adversarial.

show full answer

For context: developers used JFrog to pull in packages, check them, and use them in code. AppSec oversaw multiple projects, and we were going through a merger, so everyone had a lot of work. As an intern, I was in charge of around 30–35 projects. That exposure was valuable — more daily hands-on work than a typical single-project intern gets.

The case that stuck with me: a team was still reusing code from a developer who had left about five years ago, and they were building on top of it. I was configuring tools, running scans manually, and pushing results back so the teams could see them directly.

One of their dependency files — checked in years ago by the previous developer — was outdated and had a 9.5 severity vulnerability that allowed remote code execution. Very bad. We had a security bar (OWASP Top 10/20, code had to meet criteria before production), and this failed it hard.

I scheduled time with the developers' manager and explained not just how to fix it, but where I'd found it and how they could see these results themselves going forward. To make things easier, I set up a connector that ran scans every hour and pushed updates every three hours. If a release was blocked, they knew exactly why. If they were making changes, they could see what was working and what wasn't.

That reduced the meeting overhead and gave them transparency. They didn't need me as a bottleneck — they could see the impact of their code changes in near real-time.

Follow-up: "Was there a time you had to make the call or escalate to your manager — for example, if something was scored as medium but you evaluated it differently?"

We typically waited before escalating, to avoid getting lost in false positives. If a release was on Friday and by Thursday afternoon the team hadn't remediated, I'd bring it up to my manager. I kept detailed notes in Jira — what I'd done, when I'd reached out, and what progress we'd made. Thursday afternoon, my manager would take it directly to their managers.

Q5

"On your resume you mention a published security research paper. What was it on, and what did you learn?" Asked by: Compliance Lead

// key insight

"Navigating IoT Security" — looking at how threats shift across the cloud, edge, and fog layers. The cloud has misconfigs and key management; the edge and fog are where outdated firmware and exposed services live. The paper's real argument is that users don't know what their smart devices are actually exposing — your home router might have anonymous FTP open to the internet and the owner has no idea.

show full answer

The paper is titled something like "Navigating IoT Security: Architectures, Emerging Threats, and Adaptive Countermeasures." The core idea: technology is everywhere, and making devices "smart" introduces new risks.

Attackers have gained initial footholds through smart thermostats and similar IoT devices. Normal household users don't always realize which devices are actually internet-connected, or what those devices expose.

We looked at the three data layers:

  • Cloud — data processed at AWS/GCP. Security is shared between provider and customer. Main risks: misconfigurations, access controls, key management.
  • Edge — processing between the device and full cloud. Lower latency but a new attack surface.
  • Fog — devices talking directly to local processing nodes. Firmware and hardware security become critical here.

At each layer, data flows differently, so the threats and countermeasures differ. A lot of home routers ship with advanced features enabled — users don't know "this FTP service is open to the internet," and that leads to real exposure.

The compliance lead brought up the casino that got hacked through a smart fish tank sensor — which is the canonical example of exactly this problem.

Motivation & self-awareness

// key insight

The first Master's (Information Systems) taught me how businesses weigh risk, cost, and functionality. But I missed the hands-on side of engineering. Security was the one place I could combine both — and every time I learn something in it, the mountain gets bigger, not smaller. That's the feeling I wanted to keep chasing.

show full answer

I started my answer by pushing back gently on the "highly educated" compliment — I'm glad I still feel like I'm not, because the more I learn, the more I realize how much I don't know.

With Computer Science, I loved how engineering teaches you: "Here are tools; here's how to solve problems." But as I was graduating, I realized I didn't fully understand how businesses use those solutions. That's why I pivoted to Information Systems — understanding where engineering solutions and business needs don't align, and where the balance is between risk, cost, and functionality.

As I did that, I realized I missed getting my hands dirty. Security was the domain that kept blowing my mind — a mountain of things to learn, and I wanted to climb that mountain. So I thought: why not another Master's? That's how I ended up in Cybersecurity Engineering.

Yes, specialization makes you a subject-matter expert — but it also closes off options. Right now I want to keep my options broad, keep learning, and narrow down later as I understand more.

Everyone I've talked to in security has a non-linear path. Their experiences eventually accumulate into the mindset that lets them find a niche. I'm still in that exploration phase, and that feels like the right place to be.

How I work

Q6

"What's your approach to learning or getting up to speed on a new technology you're not familiar with? AI, for example, changes daily." Asked by: Director of Continuous Controls Monitoring

// key insight

Pomodoro structure — 25 minutes of focused learning, 5 minutes to verbalize what I just learned as if I were explaining it to a five-year-old. If I can't explain it simply, I haven't learned it yet, and I know exactly where the gap is. Reading and writing aren't the same as being able to talk about it.

show full answer

I use a Pomodoro-style approach: 25 minutes focused, 5-minute break. My process:

  1. Find a structured resource — I usually start on YouTube to see if someone has laid out a good pathway for the topic.
  2. Focus on fundamentals during that 25-minute block.
  3. In the 5-minute "break," instead of switching tasks, I try to verbalize what I just learned. Can I explain it like I'm five? If yes, I feel I understand it. If not, I've just identified my gap — I go back and take better notes.

For me, reading and writing are different from being able to verbalize. If I can't explain it clearly, I don't believe I truly understand it.

My notes can get very descriptive, but later I can pull up OneNote, Ctrl+F keywords, and find my own explanations in my own words. That makes recall easier because I've paraphrased and internalized it rather than copying someone else's wording.

Q7

"You did malware sandboxing at the AI & Cybersecurity Center at Auburn. Did you leverage AI there? Are you using AI agents in your workflow?" Asked by: Compliance Lead

// key insight

No — and I was deliberate about it. LLMs recombine existing knowledge. Malware starts as someone's novel idea, then becomes malicious. If I'm going to analyze something no model has seen, I need my own mental model of why specific values are used and how the code flows. Relying on an LLM for the walkthrough means I'd never actually learn to deal with the unknown.

show full answer

I play with AI models, yes. But I did not use AI in my malware sandbox work.

The reason: AI might give me helpful information, but I wouldn't understand where it came from. For malware analysis, I want to know why specific values are used, how the code flows, and what each routine is doing.

Historically, malware started as someone saying "I can do something cool" — think old MS-DOS demos — and then turned malicious. In the future I might analyze malware no model has seen before. If I can't understand the methodology on my own, relying on an AI model doesn't help me deal with the unknown. LLMs don't invent truly new concepts; they recombine existing knowledge. For novel malicious behavior, I'd still need my own understanding.

I haven't played much with AI agents yet because I don't fully understand how they work. From a security mindset, agents are just another technology to scrutinize and protect. And with things like AI in browsers, the privacy and monitoring concerns are huge.

I want to keep up with AI, but not get so absorbed in it that I forget core fundamentals. My balance is: stay current, don't over-rely, be mindful of new risks AI introduces.

Q8

"In cybersecurity you're often in situations with more to do than you can handle. Can you talk about a time when that happened and how you approached it?" Asked by: Director of Architecture

// key insight

GTA for 120 students as the only GTA, 4 classes, co-leading an ethical hacking club, traveling for CTFs. Every morning I ask: "What's the most important thing today?" That's my high-priority. Everything else is medium or low. If my calendar has open space, I assume I'm forgetting something — if it's not on the calendar, it won't happen.

show full answer

I start with prioritization: what needs my absolute focus right now?

Current snapshot:

  • GTA for ~120 students — I'm the only GTA, and they're graduating seniors.
  • Enrolled in 4 classes.
  • Helping lead an ethical hacking club, flying to Chicago for a CTF competition.

I often end up stretched thin. To manage it I:

  • Ask myself every morning: "What's the most important thing I need to do today?" That becomes my high priority. Everything else is medium or low.
  • Try to finish assignments ahead of time so they're off my mind. Before the CTF trip, I finished assignments through November 20th so I could focus entirely on the competition and teaching.
  • Rely heavily on my calendar and to-do list for accountability.

If I suddenly feel like I have free time, I assume I'm forgetting something. If it's not on the calendar, there's a good chance it won't happen. I even put reminders like "Call family" in there to make sure personal things don't slip.

Honest reflection

Q9

"Can you tell me about a time you made a mistake or a decision that turned out wrong? What did you do to rectify or learn from it?" Asked by: Director of Architecture

// key insight

An hour before this interview, actually. I was scheduled to present a research paper next week; three other students were ahead of me today. They didn't show. The professor asked if I could go. My slides were 3-pages-in. I said yes — but told him upfront which sections I understood deeply and which math I didn't. Transparency didn't make me look weak; it let me still deliver value and kept me honest about where I was. Hiding the gap would've hurt me more.

show full answer

I make mistakes every day. I see it as an engineering mindset — you will make mistakes; what matters is whether you learn from them.

A very recent example: about an hour before this interview, in my Malware Representation class. I was supposed to present a research paper the following week (November 18). Three other students were scheduled to present before me today.

I had read the paper. I'd started slides — about three of them were done.

None of the three showed up. The professor asked if I was ready to present today. I said no, I wasn't. But I believe being transparent about mistakes goes a long way. I told him I had partial slides but had read the paper, and asked if I could still present.

He said yes. I presented. It wasn't perfect — I clearly pointed out which sections I deeply understood and openly admitted which math sections I didn't fully understand.

That transparency:

  • Created a good status quo — I wasn't pretending; my classmates saw exactly where I stood.
  • Let me still deliver value and practice presenting under pressure.
  • Showed that hiding information would only hurt me; honesty gives you a chance to course-correct.

The lesson going forward: avoid bad assumptions ("those three will definitely go before me") and be more prepared for unknowns — but also keep being transparent when mistakes happen.

Turning the Tables — My Questions to Them

Most interview writeups skip this section. I think that's the mistake. The questions you ask tell the panel just as much about you as the answers you give them. They also give you real information about whether you want the job. Here's what I asked, and the moment that genuinely changed how I thought about the team.

MyQ

"What brought each of you to Cox, and how would you describe your journey so far?"

// why i asked

You learn more about a team from how people describe their paths than from any formal answer to "what's the culture like?" Two of them had 10-year runs across multiple Cox divisions — that told me this is a place people stay and move around in, not a place people churn out of.

MyQ

"What would success look like at week 2, week 6, and week 10 of this internship?"

// what i learned

The Director was honest: the internship is deliberately flexible so it can tune to my interests and what's happening that summer. Success is less about a checklist and more about relationships built, presence felt, and whether I'd be someone they'd consider for a longer-term role. That answer told me they actually think about interns as pipeline, not labor.

MyQ

"If you could change something about Cox, what would it be?"

// what i learned

The most direct answer came from the Director of Continuous Controls Monitoring. He said the culture is healthy and respectful, but consensus-driven — and in security, consensus can cost you speed. That's the kind of answer you only get from someone who's been there long enough to love the place and still tell you the truth about it. I trusted the whole team more after that.

MyQ

"When was the last time you or the team failed at something? Was there a security initiative that wasn't as successful as expected?"

// the answer that stuck with me

The Director of Continuous Controls Monitoring told a story about a DLP implementation — technically flawless, functionally a failure.

"We spent about $2M implementing a leading-edge DLP tool. The technical rollout went extremely well. But there was no organizational appetite to actually enforce DLP as a policy — no blocking, no follow-through on incidents, no compliance obligation forcing it. Technically a success. Functionally a failure. The lesson: always ask why are we doing this? before you build it." Director of Continuous Controls Monitoring, Cox Automotive

That story is the single most valuable thing I took away from the whole interview. You can do everything right technically and still produce zero security outcomes if the business case was never real to begin with. I'll think about that every time a "cool project" lands in front of me.

Key Takeaways

// if you're prepping for a final-round security panel
  • Storytelling beats depth. The panel told me directly at the end: "Get good at telling a story." You're often talking to people for whom security is priority #13. Get the context, the why, and the ask into 30 seconds, not 3 minutes.
  • Transparency isn't weakness. My "I'm not fully ready but here's what I know" moment in class an hour before the interview became my best answer of the day. Honesty about gaps beats performing confidence you don't have — every time.
  • Have a learning system you can explain. Nobody asks "are you a self-learner?" anymore — they assume it. What they actually want to hear is the mechanics. Pomodoro plus "explain it back to yourself" is mine. Have yours.
  • Don't fake a specialization you haven't earned. I was asked twice to pick a subdomain and twice to name one I disliked. "I don't have enough experience to answer that yet" landed better than a rehearsed answer would have. Know when to say you don't know.
  • Your questions for them matter more than you think. The failure-story question pulled the most valuable insight of the whole hour. Ask something that forces a real answer, not "what's the culture like?"

What Helped Me Prep

Nothing exotic. This interview rewarded thinking clearly about my own experience more than grinding technical prep. What I leaned on:

  • My Jira and OneNote archives from the LPL internship. The specific vulnerability story I told for Q1 came directly from notes I wrote when I was actually fixing it. If you keep notes during internships, you'll have stories forever.
  • Re-reading my own IoT paper before the call. It had been months. I was rusty on the fog/edge distinction until I skimmed the abstract again.
  • Writing out my own questions beforehand. The four I asked weren't improvised. I wrote them the night before and picked which to use based on how the interview was going.
